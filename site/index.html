The gap

Every existing option makes you choose.

Keep secrets near your code and you trade away access control, rotation, or your independence from a vendor.

✕git-crypt

Encrypts marked files, but the access list is “whoever has a key in the repo.” No notion of who can read which branch, no rotation, no portable audit of grants.

✕SOPS

Encrypts values to recipients you maintain by hand in .sops.yaml. No branch-scoped access, no verifiable history of who was granted what.

✕Vault / Doppler

Strong — but they pull secrets out of the repo into a SaaS. You swap “secret in git” for a hard dependency, and the policy lives in their database, not with your code.

The wedge

Recipients are derived from who can read the branch.

Grant someone read access to staging and they can decrypt staging’s secrets. You never maintain two lists. Revoke them and rotate re-encrypts to the new reader set.

01 / MARK

Mark the secrets

Paths in .gitattributes (.env, *.pem, secrets/**) get the gitsafe filter — committed, so they travel with the repo.

02 / CLEAN

Encrypt on add

The clean filter verifies the signed policy, resolves the current branch’s readers, and encrypts with age to exactly those keys.

03 / SMUDGE

Decrypt on checkout

Authorized identities get plaintext. Everyone else gets a clear locked placeholder — the checkout never breaks, the secret never leaks.

The guarantees

Verified before trusted.

The encrypt path refuses to act on a policy it can’t verify — so you can’t be tricked into encrypting a secret to an attacker’s key.

Signed, chained policy

Keyring and grants live as an ed25519-signed object chain committed in the repo. Every version is signed by an admin and links to its parent by hash — tamper with any of it and verification fails.

Offline trust anchor

Each clone pins the policy root (TOFU, like SSH known_hosts). A wholesale root replacement — which would otherwise verify as consistent — is detected and refused.

Private keys stay out

Identities live in ~/.config/gitsafe and never enter the repo. The policy carries only public keys.

No false confidence

Tested with unit, fuzz, race, and real-git end-to-end suites — and the threat model states exactly what is and isn’t protected, including read-after-revocation.

Quickstart

Protect your first secret in four lines.

$ gitsafe key gen                 # one-time: your private identity
$ gitsafe init --user alice       # wire filters + bootstrap policy
$ echo "API_KEY=sk-live-…" > .env
$ git add .env && git commit -m "encrypt secret"

# add a teammate, branch-scoped:
$ gitsafe member add bob --sign <hex> --enc age1…
$ gitsafe grant bob read staging
$ gitsafe rotate                   # re-encrypt to the new reader set
Status — v0.1, hardened core. CLI, clean/smudge filters, signed policy chain, branch-scoped recipients, root-pinned offline verification, and rotation are implemented and covered by unit, fuzz, race, and real-git end-to-end tests. Not yet externally audited.
